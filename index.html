About the Workshop

Long-term memory is becoming a core component of modern AI systems. Conversational assistants and agentic systems are increasingly expected to retain information across sessions, personalize to users, reason over long horizons, and act consistently across tasks, tools, and modalities. This shift moves AI systems beyond single-context interactions toward persistent memory layers that encode, retrieve, update, and sometimes forget past experience.

PALM is a workshop on safe, personalized, and long-term memory for AI agents and conversational assistants. The workshop brings together researchers working on memory architectures, personalization, multimodal and embodied memory, agentic and multi-agent memory, cognitive and neuroscience-inspired memory, evaluation, and safety. Our goal is to build a shared research agenda around how AI systems should remember, what they should forget, how memory should be evaluated, and how persistent memory can be made controllable, transparent, and safe.

02

Topics of Interest

01

Memory Architectures for Conversational Assistants

Writing, retrieving, updating, consolidating, and forgetting memories across sessions — memory stores for multi-session dialogue, retrieval policies for user preferences, consolidation from histories, and handling stale or contradictory memories.

02

Memory for LLM Agents & Multi-Agent Systems

Memory for long-horizon tasks, tool use, collaboration, and coordination — persistent task histories, tool-use traces, shared memory, provenance, isolation, and propagating or restricting memories across agents.

03

Multimodal, Visual, Video & Embodied Memory

Memory across text, images, video, audio, sensorimotor streams, and robotics — long-term video memory, visual retrieval, spatial memory, multimodal event memory, and memory for AR/VR or robotic assistants.

04

Neuroscience-Inspired & Cognitive Memory Models

Human- and biology-inspired memory design — complementary learning systems, episodic-to-semantic consolidation, replay, forgetting, abstraction, cognitive maps, and human vs. machine memory limitations.

05

Benchmarking & Evaluation

Evaluating memory beyond short-context recall — long-horizon benchmarks, temporal reasoning over past events, update and deletion tests, contradiction handling, abstention, and oracle-retrieval comparisons.

06

Safety, Privacy & Security

Risks of persistent memory and personalization — memory poisoning, prompt injection through stored memories, sleeper memories, privacy and cross-domain leakage, sycophancy, harmful belief reinforcement, and alignment drift.

07

User Control & Transparency

Understanding and controlling what AI systems remember — interfaces for inspecting, editing, deleting, and scoping memories; consent and access control; provenance; and right-to-be-forgotten mechanisms.

Call for Papers

We invite submissions on long-term memory for AI agents, conversational assistants, and personalized AI systems — from machine learning, NLP, AI agents, HCI, cognitive science, neuroscience, privacy, security, and AI safety. Submissions may present new architectures, benchmarks, datasets, evaluations, systems, theoretical perspectives, position papers, negative results, or interdisciplinary analyses.

Submission tracks

Full-Length Papers

Up to 9 pages, excluding references and supplementary materials.

Short Papers

Up to 4 pages, excluding references and supplementary materials.

Format & policies

  • Template. Use the NeurIPS 2026 template, submitted as a PDF.
  • Non-archival & dual submission. Welcomes ongoing and unpublished work, as well as papers under review or recently accepted elsewhere (provided this does not breach the other venue's dual-submission or anonymity policies). Work may be submitted elsewhere concurrently or subsequently.
  • Double-blind review. Submissions must be fully anonymized, including any supplementary or linked material such as code. Violations may be desk-rejected.
  • Visibility. Accepted papers will be made public; rejected submissions and reviews will not.
  • LLM usage. AI-generated papers are not allowed. AI assistance is permitted, but submissions must be primarily human-authored, reflecting original thought and analysis.
